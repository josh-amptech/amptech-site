# Hallway Usability Report

Date: 2026-03-23
Site reviewed: http://localhost:3000/
Method: 10 simulated hallway-test personas reviewed the live site and completed the same post-review survey. This is directional feedback, not a substitute for moderated tests with real prospects.

## Survey Used

Scaled questions:

1. First impression quality: very poor, poor, neutral, good, very good
2. Messaging clarity: very unclear, unclear, neutral, clear, very clear
3. Value proposition relevance to you: very low, low, neutral, high, very high
4. Trust and credibility: very low, low, neutral, high, very high
5. Ease of understanding the next step: very difficult, difficult, neutral, easy, very easy
6. Likelihood to book a discovery call: very unlikely, unlikely, neutral, likely, very likely

Freeform questions:

- What did you like most about the site?
- What did you like least about the site?
- What is one thing you would improve?
- What, if anything, would stop you from contacting this company?

## Personas Tested

1. Small local business owner from Google
2. Marketing manager at a growing B2B company from a referral
3. Startup founder looking for an MVP from Google
4. Nonprofit operations lead from a referral
5. Restaurant and hospitality owner from Google
6. Ecommerce manager looking for conversion improvements
7. Local professional services owner from a referral
8. Internal product lead at a mid-sized company from Google
9. Funded SaaS founder from a referral
10. Small business owner with limited technical knowledge from Google

## Aggregate Results

### Scaled responses

| Question | Result |
| --- | --- |
| First impression quality | 10 of 10 rated `good` |
| Messaging clarity | 9 of 10 rated `clear`, 1 of 10 rated `very clear` |
| Value proposition relevance | 2 `high`, 2 `neutral`, 5 `low`, 1 `very low` |
| Trust and credibility | 9 `neutral`, 1 `high` |
| Ease of understanding next step | 10 of 10 rated `very easy` |
| Likelihood to book a discovery call | 3 `likely`, 1 `neutral`, 5 `unlikely`, 1 `very unlikely` |

### High-level read

The current homepage is performing well on presentation, clarity, and CTA comprehension. It is underperforming on perceived proof and relevance for visitors who are not already looking for an app or MVP partner.

The page appears well-tuned for founder and product-led traffic:

- Startup founders and SaaS buyers responded positively to the promise of speed plus senior engineering judgment.
- Visitors interested in custom software were more willing to book.

The page appears weak for broader search and referral traffic:

- Website redesign buyers did not see themselves in the offer.
- Service-business visitors often interpreted the site as too specialized.
- Multiple testers assumed the business primarily serves founders with app ideas, not general business websites or growth work.

## Repeated Strengths

- The site feels polished, intentional, and professional.
- The messaging is easy to understand quickly.
- The CTA is obvious and the next step is clear.
- The process section reduces uncertainty.
- The ownership and clean handoff messaging is reassuring.

## Repeated Weaknesses

- There is not enough proof on the page.
- The positioning feels too narrow for broad search traffic.
- Visitors looking for website help do not feel like the intended customer.
- There are few concrete examples, outcomes, or recognizable trust signals.

## Main Findings

### 1. Clarity is strong, but relevance is narrow

Most testers understood the message immediately. The problem is not confusion. The problem is that many visitors concluded the offer was for someone else.

This pattern was strongest among:

- restaurant and hospitality buyers
- small-business website buyers
- professional services firms
- nonprofit website-refresh traffic
- ecommerce operators looking for business results

### 2. The CTA path is working

Every tester understood what to do next. The contact path is not the main blocker.

The current conversion constraint is more likely to be:

- audience fit
- message-market fit
- missing proof

not button placement or next-step clarity.

### 3. Credibility is acceptable, but not persuasive yet

Most testers landed on neutral trust rather than high trust. The site reads as competent, but not yet proven.

The repeated request was for:

- case studies
- client logos
- measurable outcomes
- screenshots or examples of shipped work
- testimonials or references with more specificity

### 4. The page strongly favors founder and SaaS traffic

Founder-oriented testers were the most likely to book. They responded well to:

- "built in weeks, not months"
- "20+ years of engineering experience"
- "you own the code"
- the explicit bridge between AI speed and engineering judgment

If that is the intended audience, the homepage direction is broadly correct. It mainly needs stronger proof.

If the business also wants to capture broader "website help" traffic, the homepage currently filters too aggressively.

## Representative Observations

- "The site is clear, confident, and easy to navigate."
- "It feels more focused on app startups than on general small-business web work."
- "The CTA is obvious, the contact form is simple, and the process is spelled out."
- "I want more concrete proof, like examples, outcomes, or a stronger portfolio signal."
- "If I came in looking for a standard business website, I'd probably bounce because the offer feels too specialized."

## Conversion Interpretation

### Best-fit traffic

The current homepage is best aligned to:

- startup founders with app ideas
- SaaS founders seeking delivery help
- product leaders looking for a focused app or MVP partner

### Weak-fit traffic

The current homepage is weaker for:

- general website redesign buyers
- local service businesses
- buyers seeking marketing outcomes rather than software delivery
- referral visitors who expect an agency-style portfolio and broader service list

## Recommendations

### Highest priority

1. Add proof near the top of the homepage.
   - Include 2 to 3 concrete proof points within the first half of the page.
   - Prioritize case studies, logos, screenshots, measurable results, or direct client evidence.

2. Make target audience fit explicit.
   - Decide whether the homepage is intentionally founder-first.
   - If yes, state that even more explicitly and let poor-fit traffic self-select.
   - If no, broaden the messaging to include business website and digital product buyers.

3. Add examples that map to multiple buyer types.
   - Show at least one example for founder or SaaS traffic.
   - Show at least one example for a more traditional business buyer if that audience matters.

### Secondary priority

4. Clarify the kinds of projects AmpTech does and does not take on.
   - This reduces hesitation for qualified visitors and bounce from poor-fit visitors.

5. Add outcome-based language alongside engineering language.
   - Some buyers care less about the build method and more about launch speed, lead generation, internal efficiency, or revenue impact.

6. Consider audience-specific landing pages for search or referral traffic.
   - Founder and MVP build
   - Business website refresh
   - Industry-specific pages where relevant

## Proposed Follow-up Stories

1. Add a homepage proof section with case studies, outcomes, and trust signals.
2. Refine the homepage hero and supporting copy to clarify whether AmpTech is founder-first or broader.
3. Create audience-specific landing pages for non-founder traffic if that audience remains in scope.
4. Validate updated messaging with real human testers from founder and non-founder segments.

## Bottom Line

The homepage is doing a good job of signaling quality and a clear path to action. It is not doing enough to prove the claims or widen perceived relevance beyond founder-style app traffic.

If the primary audience is founders and product buyers, keep the overall direction and add proof.

If the business also wants more general search and referral traffic for website and business-facing work, the messaging and examples need to broaden or segment.
